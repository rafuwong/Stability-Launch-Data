Why do we want to use pandas over excel?¶

Many companies use python language with Pandas dataframe to conduct backend data collection and calculations Also used by the scientific, financial, educational, engineering fields for data analysis.

Reproducibility¶

  • Pandas can read and convert to many types of files (csv, txt, json, and even excel files).
  • Can be used to read not only stability launch data but other data as well.

Scalability¶

  • Pandas dataframe is one of the fastest data analysis tools because it also utilizes dask.
  • Can read as much data as the memory (RAM) on a computer/laptop permits. Versus excel that only reads ~1 million rows.
  • Dataframe structure allows for quick and easy analysis of data through other languages and platforms.

Educational and Presentation Purposes¶

  • Anyone can generate graphs, reports, and spreadsheets/calculators with a click of a button for quick reports.
  • A quick way to summarize our stability launch process which in turn will:
    • Help educate the lab about our process so they know how they can better fit into our process.
    • Help educate new sample management employees about our stability launch process.
    • Gives us the ability to look into and question our processes to find gaps and further improve our process.
    • Can give us the ability to look into relogs, chamber reconiliation, and analysis of other processes.

Coolness factor¶

  • We get to use machine learning algorithms that are utilized by leading tech companies of the world.
  • All of the data analysis will be automated
  • Generate diverse sets of graphical data.

What does the correlation heatmap tell us?¶

  • The closer to the value of 1 means the two metrics have a strong linear relationship.
  • Tell us any metrics that are redundant (also known as overfitting) for modeling
    • Example: Label number and container number
  • Shows us the important features to utilize as the training set for machine learning.

What can we observe from this heatmap?¶

Container count:¶

- Not necessary as label count gives us a better correlation with log-in, labeling, verifying.
- It does give us a better correlation to our launch times.

Log-in time:¶

- Is moderately correlated to label count, review time, and total time.

Label time:¶

- Is moderately correlated with log-in time.
- Is near perfect correlation with label count.

Verify time:¶

- Is moderately correlated with log-in time.
- Near perfect correlation with label time.

Launch time:¶

- Is moderately correlated with container number and review time

  • Median log-in time for linked ~1.5 hours:
    • 50% of linked launches take longer than 1.5 hours to log in
  • Medians are fairly different so it is safe to say that linked samples have a high chance of affecting log-in time.

Summary of how to estimate work hours for each launch:¶

  • Linked Samples: If linked samples are present estimate 1 to 1.5 hours for log in time (average). Lots of outliers for unlinked sample log-in.

  • Multiple Orientations: If there are multiple orientations for a launch then increase estimated/average time for labeling and verification.

  • Delays: Delays can/will possibly throw off the estimation model. (slow down rate or unpredictable)

Let's move onto the machine learning model linear regression. (Ordinary least squares)¶

Pros: fast, no tuning of costants, highly interpretable, well-understood math, great fit for our data

Cons: This is assuming that our data follows a linear regression.
